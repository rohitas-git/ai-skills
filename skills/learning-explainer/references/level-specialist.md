# Level: Specialist / Practitioner

## Purpose
Explain concepts to people who work with or regularly use the topic in professional or practical contexts. Focus on application, workflows, trade-offs, and real-world implementation.

## Audience Characteristics
- Has working knowledge of related concepts
- Needs practical, actionable understanding
- Cares about how things are actually done
- Interested in tools, methods, common problems, and best practices
- Values efficiency and avoiding common mistakes

## Goals
- Provide clear, professional-level understanding
- Highlight practical applications and workflows
- Point out common pitfalls and how to avoid them
- Discuss tools, techniques, and decision factors

## Vocabulary & Style Guidelines
- Professional but accessible language
- Use domain terms (explained briefly if borderline)
- Focus on "how to" and "what works in practice"
- Include trade-offs and real constraints

## Recommended Structure
1. **Practical overview** (what it is used for)
2. **Core mechanism** (how it actually works in practice)
3. **Typical workflow** or process
4. **Key tools / methods** used
5. **Common challenges & pitfalls** + solutions
6. **Decision factors** (when to use what)

## Example (Photosynthesis – for a biologist or agronomist)
"Photosynthesis is the biochemical process by which photoautotrophs convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.

In practice: Light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes (producing ATP and NADPH), while the Calvin cycle in the stroma fixes CO₂ into sugars. Efficiency is typically 1–2% in C3 plants and higher in C4 plants.

Workflow in agriculture: Understanding photosynthetic efficiency helps in crop selection, optimizing light exposure, managing water stress, and breeding for higher-yielding varieties under climate stress.

Common pitfalls: Overlooking photorespiration in C3 crops, ignoring stomatal limitations under drought, or assuming all plants respond the same to increased CO₂.

Tools: Chlorophyll fluorescence meters, gas exchange systems (LI-COR), and modeling software (e.g., Farquhar–von Caemmerer–Berry model)."
