# Mental Models and Diagrams

## Purpose
Help learners build strong, memorable mental representations of concepts. Mental models and diagrams reduce cognitive load and improve long-term understanding and transfer.

## Why Mental Models Matter
Good mental models allow people to:
- Predict what will happen
- Explain why something works
- Apply the idea to new situations
- Remember the concept more easily

## Types of Mental Models (Choose Based on Level)
- **Simple Analogy** (Child / Beginner): "Plant is like a solar kitchen"
- **System / Factory Model** (Layperson / Specialist): Inputs → Process → Outputs + Waste
- **Flowchart / Process Model**: Step-by-step sequence with decision points
- **Causal Loop / Feedback Diagram**: How parts influence each other over time
- **Matrix / Comparison Table**: Compare options, pathways, or trade-offs
- **Hierarchical / Layered Model**: Different levels of organization (molecular → cellular → organism → ecosystem)
- **Z-Scheme / Energy Diagram** (Academic / Frontier): Specific scientific visualizations

## Diagram Guidelines by Level
- **Child**: Very simple icons + story-like arrows. Heavy use of familiar objects.
- **Beginner**: Clean labeled diagram with 3–5 elements max.
- **Layperson**: Clear flowchart or before/after comparison.
- **Specialist**: Workflow diagrams, decision trees, or tool/process maps.
- **Academic**: Formal models, equations alongside diagrams, or established scientific schematics (e.g., Z-scheme).
- **Frontier**: Systems-level diagrams, proposed new models, or comparison of competing frameworks.

## Recommended Output Formats
1. **Text description** of the mental model (always)
2. **Mermaid code** for flowcharts, mind maps, or diagrams (preferred when possible)
3. **ASCII art** for simple cases
4. **Offer to generate image** using Grok Imagine when a rich visual would help

## How to Introduce a Mental Model
- Name it clearly: "A useful mental model here is..."
- Explain the mapping: "In this analogy, X corresponds to Y because..."
- Highlight limitations: "Of course, real photosynthesis is more complex than a kitchen..."

## Best Practices
- Use **one strong model** per explanation rather than many weak ones.
- Make the model **active** — show how it helps predict or explain something.
- Adapt complexity to the audience level.
- When moving to deeper levels, evolve or replace the model with a more sophisticated one.
- Always connect the model back to the original concept.

## CLT Connection
Mental models and diagrams are powerful tools for **reducing extraneous load** (by offloading working memory to visuals) and **increasing germane load** (by helping learners build integrated schemas). Use them deliberately, especially at lower levels.
